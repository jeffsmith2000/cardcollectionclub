Pokémon 151 One Year Later — Price Trends

By John C · February 18, 2026 · 9 min read

When Pokémon 151 (sv3pt5) dropped in September 2023, the hype was absolutely unreal. A set celebrating the original 151 Kanto Pokémon, featuring stunning Illustration Rares of every starter, every legendary, and fan favourites like Gengar, Snorlax, and Eevee? It was tailor-made for the nostalgia-driven collector market. But now, well over a year later, how have prices actually held up?

Let's dig into the data.

The Big Picture

Pokémon 151 has defied the typical post-release price curve. Most modern sets see their chase cards peak within the first 2–4 weeks, then decline as supply floods the market. 151 did experience an initial dip, but prices stabilised faster than expected and have been climbing steadily since mid-2024. The Kanto nostalgia factor appears to be providing a durable price floor that other modern sets simply don't have.

The Winners

Charizard ex SAR (#199)

No surprises here. The Charizard Special Art Rare has climbed from ~$250 to ~$320 AUD and shows no signs of stopping. Every Charizard card from a significant set becomes a blue-chip collectible, and 151's Charizard is no exception. The artwork — featuring Charizard soaring over Kanto — is genuinely iconic.

Gengar Illustration Rare (#176)

The sleeper hit of 151. Gengar's IR was initially overshadowed by the big-name SARs, but quiet demand from Gengar fans and horror-aesthetic collectors has pushed it from $18 to $28 AUD — a 56% increase. Gengar consistently outperforms expectations across every set it appears in.

Venusaur & Blastoise ex SARs

The other two Kanto starters have been climbing steadily as collectors aim to complete the trio. Venusaur (#200) jumped 42% to $85 AUD, and Blastoise (#202) matched that pace at $78 AUD. Expect these to continue rising as "complete set" demand drives purchases.

Alakazam ex SAR (#201)

A genuine surprise performer. Alakazam was always popular among Gen 1 fans, but its 44% price increase suggests deeper demand than many expected. The psychic-themed artwork resonated strongly with the nostalgia crowd.

The Underperformers

Pikachu Illustration Rare (#172)

Interestingly, the Pikachu IR has actually dipped slightly from $30 to $25 AUD. This is likely due to oversaturation — there are so many Pikachu cards across so many sets that no single one commands a dramatic premium unless it's truly exceptional. The 151 Pikachu IR is lovely, but it's competing with dozens of other Pikachu chase cards.

Full Art ex Cards (Non-SAR)

The standard Full Art ex cards (not Illustration Rares or Special Art Rares) have mostly declined 10–20% from release prices. This is normal for modern sets — the mid-tier cards absorb selling pressure as collectors focus on the top-end chase cards.

Sealed product has been the real star of 151's post-release performance. Japanese booster bundles have more than doubled in value, driven by the Master Ball reverse holo pattern exclusive to Japanese product. The English Ultra Premium Collection has also performed strongly, sitting at $260 AUD — a 44% increase from its $180 retail price.

Key Insight: Japanese 151 sealed product has significantly outperformed English equivalents in percentage terms. The combination of lower initial cost, exclusive Master Ball pattern, and perceived scarcity has made Japanese 151 one of the best sealed investments of the modern era.

What's Driving the Prices?

  • Nostalgia is unbeatable: Gen 1 Pokémon have universal recognition. The 151 set taps into childhood memories for an entire generation of collectors.
  • Print run concerns: 151 was a subset (sv3pt5), meaning it had a shorter print window than mainline sets. Supply is tightening.
  • Set completeness appeal: Having exactly 151 Pokémon in the set creates a compelling "gotta catch 'em all" completionist drive that boosts demand across the entire set, not just chase cards.
  • Cross-generation appeal: Both original 90s fans and current-generation Pokémon fans connect with these characters.

Looking Ahead

We expect Pokémon 151 to continue appreciating over the next 2–3 years, with the SARs and Japanese sealed product leading the way. The set has all the hallmarks of a modern classic: beloved characters, stunning artwork, limited print window, and broad market appeal.

If you've been holding 151 product — sealed or singles — you're in a great position. If you haven't bought in yet, current prices still represent reasonable entry points, particularly for Japanese sealed product and the non-Charizard SARs which still have significant room to grow.

151 was special from day one. A year later, the numbers prove it. 🔴🔵
