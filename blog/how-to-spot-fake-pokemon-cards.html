How to Spot Fake Pokemon Cards: Complete Authentication Guide

J
John Ceron
February 7, 2026 · 15 min read

Fake Pokemon cards are everywhere. Facebook Marketplace, eBay, Gumtree, conventions, even some dodgy retail stores. As card values have skyrocketed, so has the sophistication of counterfeits. I've personally caught fakes at card shows, in trades, and in online purchases — and I've talked to plenty of collectors who weren't so lucky.

This guide covers every authentication method I use, from quick 5-second checks to detailed forensic examination. Learn these and you'll never get burned.

The Quick Tests (5-30 seconds)

These are your first line of defence. If a card fails any of these, it's almost certainly fake.

1. The Texture Test

Easy — No Tools Needed

What to do: Run your fingernail or fingertip across the front surface of any holo, full art, V, VMAX, VSTAR, ex, or illustration rare card.

What you should feel: Real modern Pokemon cards (2017 onwards) with any kind of special treatment have a distinct textured surface. You'll feel tiny ridges and grooves under your fingernail. The texture follows the artwork — it's not a uniform pattern. On full art cards, the texture extends across the entire card face. On regular holos, the holo window area has texture.

Red flag: If the card is perfectly smooth and glossy, it's fake. This is the single most reliable quick test. Fake manufacturers can replicate colours, fonts, and even holo patterns, but the textured surface is extremely difficult and expensive to counterfeit. As of 2026, almost no fakes get the texture right.

Exception: Older cards (pre-Sun & Moon era) and non-holo cards won't have texture. For Base Set through XY era, you'll need other tests.

2. The Light Test

Easy — Phone Torch Required

What to do: Turn on your phone's flashlight and hold the card directly in front of it, so the light shines through from behind.

What you should see: Real Pokemon cards have a thin black core layer sandwiched between the front and back card stock. When you hold a real card up to light, you should see a dim, muted glow — the light passes through, but it's heavily filtered by that black layer. You shouldn't be able to clearly see any artwork or text through the card.

Red flag: If the card lets bright light through and you can clearly see the artwork from behind, the black core layer is missing. This means the card is printed on regular cardstock, not authentic Pokemon TCG card stock. Instant fake.

Also check: The light should appear relatively uniform across the card. If you see bright spots or uneven light transmission, the card stock is inconsistent — another fake indicator.

3. The Rip Test (Destructive — Last Resort)

Easy — But Destroys the Card

What to do: Tear the card in half and look at the cross-section.

What you should see: A real Pokemon card has a visible black line running through the middle of the card stock when viewed from the side. This is the same black core visible in the light test.

When to use: Only on bulk/worthless cards to verify a batch. If someone is selling you 1,000 bulk cards, rip one to check. Never rip a card with value. This test is listed for completeness — the light test gives you the same information without destruction.

The Detailed Tests (1-5 minutes)

For higher-value purchases or when you're not 100% sure from the quick tests.

4. Colour Saturation & Print Quality

Medium — Comparison Card Helpful

What to check: Compare the suspect card to a known authentic card from the same set (or any card you're 100% sure is real).

Fake tells:

  • Over-saturated colours: Fakes often have colours that are "too vivid" — the yellows are too bright, the reds too deep, the blues too electric. Real Pokemon cards have a specific, slightly muted colour palette.
  • Washed-out colours: Some fakes go the other direction — everything looks faded or slightly grey, like a bad photocopy.
  • Blue tint on the back: The back of a Pokemon card should have a specific shade of blue. Fakes frequently get this wrong — too dark, too light, or with a purple/cyan tint. Compare backs side by side.
  • Pixelation: Under a loupe or magnifying glass, fakes show visible pixels or dot patterns, especially in the artwork. Real cards use professional printing that appears smooth under magnification.
  • Colour bleeding: On fakes, colours sometimes bleed into adjacent areas. Borders aren't as crisp. The edges of text might look fuzzy rather than sharp.

5. Font & Text Analysis

Medium — Loupe Recommended

What to check: Look closely at the font used for the card name, HP, attack descriptions, and set information at the bottom.

Fake tells:

  • Wrong font weight: The card name font on fakes is often slightly too thin or too bold compared to real cards.
  • Spacing issues: Letter spacing (kerning) is frequently wrong on fakes. Letters might be too close together or too spread out.
  • © symbol and set info: The tiny copyright text at the bottom of the card is one of the hardest things for fakers to replicate. Look for incorrect years, misspellings (commonly "Nintedo" instead of "Nintendo"), or wrong font sizing.
  • Energy symbols: The energy symbols in attack costs should be perfectly round with clear, crisp icons inside. Fakes often have slightly oval or blurry energy symbols.
  • HP text: On modern cards, the HP number should be in a specific font weight and size relative to the card name. Fakes frequently get this ratio wrong.

6. Holo Pattern Analysis

Advanced — Experience Needed

What to check: The holographic pattern on the card should match what's expected for that card's rarity and era.

Fake tells:

  • Wrong holo type: Different eras use different holo patterns. WOTC-era holos have a "galaxy" or "cosmos" pattern. Modern V/VMAX cards have a swirl texture. If the holo pattern doesn't match the era, it's wrong.
  • Uniform holo: Real holo patterns shift and move as you tilt the card. Fakes sometimes use a static holographic overlay that looks the same from every angle.
  • Holo bleed: On authentic cards, the holo effect is precisely contained within specific areas. On some fakes, the holo extends beyond where it should, bleeding into borders or text areas.
  • Rainbow effect: Modern full arts should display a specific rainbow effect when tilted. If the rainbow colours appear in the wrong order or the effect is too uniform, that's suspicious.

7. Weight & Feel

Medium — Scale Helpful

What to check: A standard Pokemon card weighs approximately 1.7-1.8 grams. Holo and full art cards can be slightly heavier (up to 1.9g) due to the foil layer.

Fake tells:

  • Too light: Fakes printed on regular paper or thin cardstock will weigh noticeably less — often under 1.5g.
  • Too heavy: Some "premium" fakes use heavier stock, pushing 2.0g+. Close but still detectably wrong.
  • Wrong flexibility: Real Pokemon cards have a specific flex to them. Hold the card between thumb and forefinger and gently bend. A real card flexes smoothly and springs back. Fakes often feel either too stiff (like thick photo paper) or too flimsy (like regular printer paper).
  • Surface finish: Real cards have a semi-matte finish on non-holo areas. Fakes are frequently too glossy (like a laminated photo) or too rough.

8. The Bend Test

Easy — No Tools Needed

What to do: Gently curve the card into a slight U-shape (don't crease it). A real card will flex and return to flat. Note the resistance and how it feels.

Fake tells: Fake cards tend to feel more rigid or more floppy — the multi-layer construction of real cards gives them a specific springiness that single-layer fakes can't replicate. If it feels like bending a playing card from a regular deck, that's actually a good sign. If it feels like bending photo paper or thin plastic, be suspicious.

Vintage-Specific Checks (WOTC Era)

Base Set through Neo era fakes are the most common because the values are highest. Extra checks for vintage:

  • Star symbol (★): The rarity star on 1st Edition holos should be a specific shape and size. Fakes frequently use a generic star character that's slightly different.
  • 1st Edition stamp: The stamp should be clean, crisp, and positioned correctly. On Base Set, it's to the left of the artwork box. The font and sizing are specific — compare to verified photos online.
  • Drop shadow: Base Set cards have a shadow border on the right and bottom edges of the artwork window. "Shadowless" prints lack this — but fake shadowless are rampant because the premium is so high. Verify all shadowless claims carefully.
  • Yellow border colour: WOTC-era English cards have a very specific shade of yellow for the border. Compare to a known real card — fakes are often too orange or too pale.

Where Fakes Show Up Most

  1. Facebook Marketplace — The #1 source of fakes in Australia. No seller verification, easy to create fake accounts, and buyers often don't check until it's too late.
  2. Gumtree — Same issues as FB Marketplace. "Found in my old room" listings that are actually bulk-ordered fakes from China.
  3. eBay (unverified sellers) — Less common due to buyer protection, but still happens. Check seller feedback and look for "proxy" or "custom" in descriptions — those are fakes being sold semi-openly.
  4. Card shows (rare) — Less common at reputable conventions but it happens. Small-time sellers at markets and fairs are the biggest risk.
  5. Retail stores (rare) — Resealed booster packs in retail stores are a known issue. If the pack seal looks wrong, the cards inside might be fakes or the valuable cards may have been swapped.

What to Do If You Bought a Fake

  • eBay: Open an INAD (Item Not As Described) case immediately. eBay buyer protection is strong and you'll almost certainly get a refund.
  • PayPal: If you paid via PayPal (including through Facebook), open a dispute. You have 180 days.
  • Facebook Marketplace: Report the seller and listing. If you paid cash in person, you're unfortunately out of luck unless you can track down the seller.
  • Bank chargeback: If all else fails and you paid by card, contact your bank about a chargeback. This should be a last resort.
  • Document everything: Photos of the fake, screenshots of the listing, messages with the seller. This evidence is crucial for disputes.
The golden rule: If the deal seems too good to be true, it is. A "mint" Base Set Charizard for $100 on Facebook Marketplace is a fake. Every single time. Don't let greed override common sense.

Your Authentication Kit

What to carry for serious buying:

  • ✅ Jeweller's loupe (10x magnification) — $15 on eBay
  • ✅ Phone torch — already in your pocket
  • ✅ A known authentic card for comparison — any real card from your collection
  • ✅ Digital scale (0.1g accuracy) — optional but useful, $10-20
  • ✅ This guide bookmarked on your phone

Stay safe out there. The card community is overwhelmingly honest and passionate, but every hobby has its bad actors. Know the tests, trust your gut, and when in doubt, walk away. There are always more cards. 🔍
