How to Sell Pokemon Cards in Australia: The Complete 2026 Guide

March 31, 2026 · 12 min read · Card Collection Club AU

Whether you've pulled a chase card, inherited a collection, or you're trimming your portfolio, knowing where and how to sell Pokemon cards in Australia makes the difference between getting 50% of value and getting 95%. This guide covers every major platform, their fees, and what works best depending on what you're selling.

Step 1: Know What You Have

Before listing anything, you need to know what your cards are actually worth. Don't guess.

  1. Check eBay sold listings — Filter by "Sold Items" and "Australia" to see what cards actually sold for (not what people are asking). This is your most reliable price reference.
  2. Use TCGPlayer or PriceCharting — Good for US market prices. Convert to AUD and add 10-20% for the Australian premium on popular cards.
  3. Grade matters — A Near Mint Umbreon VMAX Alt Art is worth $800+. A Lightly Played copy might be $500. Condition drives everything.
  4. Separate your cards into tiers:
    • High value ($50+ AUD) — Sell individually, take your time
    • Mid value ($10-$50) — Sell individually or in small lots
    • Low value (under $10) — Sell as bulk or bundles
    • Bulk commons/uncommons — Sell by weight or per-1000 lots

Step 2: Choose Your Platform

eBay Australia — Best for High-Value Singles & Graded Cards

eBay remains the king for selling Pokemon cards over $20 AUD. The audience is massive, prices are transparent, and you get buyer/seller protection.

eBay Fees (2026)

Final value fee: ~13.3% + $0.50 per sale

Payment processing: Included in final value fee

Net to seller: ~86% of sale price

Tips for eBay:

  • Use a flatbed scanner for card images — phone photos look amateur
  • List on Wednesday/Thursday evenings for weekend auction endings
  • Start auctions at $0.99 for high-value cards — they'll find their price with enough watchers
  • Always use tracked shipping with signature for cards over $100
  • Photograph front, back, and close-ups of any imperfections

Fetch TCG — Best for Singles in the AU/NZ Market

Fetch TCG is Oceania's dedicated TCG marketplace. If you're selling Pokemon, One Piece, or Yu-Gi-Oh singles, this is where the local community lives.

  • Lower fees than eBay
  • Built specifically for card sellers
  • AU/NZ focused — faster shipping, local buyers
  • Growing rapidly in 2026

Facebook Groups — Best for Quick Local Sales

Join groups like "Pokemon TCG Trades and Sales Australia" or "Pokemon Sales Australia." Zero platform fees, direct negotiation, and you can sell in-person for cash.

Watch Out for Scams

Never accept bank transfer before meeting. Use PayPal Goods & Services for shipped sales. Screenshots of PayID can be faked. Meet at a public location for in-person trades.

Whatnot — Best for Live Selling

Whatnot's live auction format has exploded in Australia. Great for modern singles, slabs, and sealed product. The entertainment factor means cards can sell above market if the audience is engaged.

  • Apply to become a seller at whatnot.com
  • Platform handles shipping labels and buyer payments
  • Best for sellers with personality who can run engaging streams
  • Hot for PSA slabs and sealed ETBs

Local Card Shops — Best for Instant Cash

Walk in, get a quote, walk out with money. No shipping, no waiting. The trade-off is that shops need margin, so expect 50-70% of market value for singles and 70-80% for sealed product.

Check our Card Shop Directory to find shops near you that buy collections.

Pro tip: Ask for store credit instead of cash. Most shops give 10-20% more in credit, and you can use it on sealed product you'd buy anyway.

Conventions & Card Shows — Best for Volume

If you've got a binder or boxes to move, conventions are gold. You're surrounded by buyers. Set up a table or walk the floor selling to vendors and collectors.

  • Table fees range from $50-$250 depending on the event
  • Expect to sell 30-60% of what you bring
  • Price cards 5-10% below eBay — buyers expect convention deals
  • Bring a card reader or have PayID ready — not everyone carries cash

Step 3: Shipping Cards Safely in Australia

Nothing kills a sale faster than a card arriving damaged. Here's how to ship properly:

  1. Sleeve the card in a penny sleeve, then into a toploader
  2. Tape the toploader opening (fold tape over so the buyer can remove it without tearing)
  3. Place between two pieces of cardboard inside a rigid mailer or padded envelope
  4. Write "DO NOT BEND" on the envelope
  5. Use tracked shipping for anything over $20 — Australia Post's small parcel tracked starts at ~$4.20
  6. Add signature on delivery for cards over $100

Shipping costs: Letter with toploader = ~$1.50 (untracked). Small tracked parcel = ~$4.20-$9.60. Express with tracking = ~$12-$15. Factor this into your pricing.

Step 4: Pricing Strategy

  • Price at 85-95% of eBay sold prices for quick sales on Facebook/direct
  • Price at 100-105% on eBay to account for fees and still match market
  • Graded cards (PSA/CGC/BGS) always sell for more — if a raw card is worth $100+ and is in clean condition, consider grading before selling
  • Be patient with high-value cards — the right buyer pays the right price. Don't fire-sale a $500 card for $300 because you're impatient.
  • Bundle mid-tier cards — "Lot of 5 Eeveelution V cards" sells faster than 5 separate $8-12 listings

Step 5: Tax Implications

If you're selling cards as a hobby, occasional sales are generally not taxed. But if you're buying with the intention to resell at a profit (which sounds like a business), the ATO may consider you a sole trader. Key thresholds:

  • Under $75K revenue — No GST registration required, but you may still owe income tax on profits
  • Over $75K revenue — Must register for GST
  • Keep records of what you paid and what you sold for — the ATO can audit card sellers
  • Talk to an accountant if you're consistently making $5K+ per year from card sales

What's Working Right Now (March 2026)

  • One Piece sealed product has strong demand, especially OP01, OP05, OP09 boxes
  • PSA 10 modern cards in the $50-200 range are the sweet spot for quick sales
  • Japanese Pokemon has a growing collector base in AU — JP exclusive cards command premiums
  • Bulk selling works best at conventions or through dedicated bulk buyers like PokéBox

Final Tips

  1. Take your time with expensive cards. The right buyer exists — you just need to find them.
  2. Diversify your selling platforms. eBay for reach, Facebook for speed, card shops for convenience.
  3. Never sell graded cards raw. If a card is worth grading, grade it first.
  4. Build a reputation. Consistent, honest selling on any platform builds repeat buyers.
