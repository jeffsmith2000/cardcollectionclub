Card Collection Insurance: Do You Need It in Australia?

By Card Collection Club | March 2026 | Collecting Advice

A house fire. A break-in. Flooding from a burst pipe. These aren't abstract risks — they're things that happen to Australian households every year. If your Pokemon or One Piece card collection is worth more than $1,000, you need to understand how Australian insurance treats collectibles and whether your current policy actually protects you.

Disclaimer: This is general information only, not financial or legal advice. Consult a qualified insurance broker for advice specific to your collection and circumstances.

📌 Key finding: Standard Australian home and contents insurance almost universally has a "collectibles" cap — typically $1,000–$3,000 regardless of actual collection value. If your collection is worth more than this, your standard policy is not adequate.

Does Home and Contents Insurance Cover Cards?

In Australia, most home and contents policies from major insurers (NRMA, Budget Direct, Allianz, QBE, Youi) include collectibles under their general contents coverage — but with strict sub-limits. Reading the Product Disclosure Statement (PDS) of common Australian policies, you'll typically find:

  • "Collectables and memorabilia: covered up to $1,000 per item, maximum $3,000 in aggregate"
  • "Coins, stamps, and trading cards: covered up to $1,500 per event"
  • "Unspecified high value items: maximum $1,000 per item unless separately listed"

Translation: if you have a $500 PSA 10 Charizard, $800 Blue Bottom sealed box, and $1,200 in various singles — and your house burns down — most standard policies will pay you $1,000–$3,000 regardless of the $2,500+ actual replacement value.

When You Definitely Need Specialist Coverage

You need to act if:

  • Your collection is worth more than $5,000
  • You have individual cards worth more than $500
  • You transport cards to conventions or shows regularly
  • You buy and sell cards as a business (personal insurance usually excludes stock)
  • You store cards at a location other than your primary residence

Options for Australian Card Collectors

1. Schedule High-Value Items on Your Home Policy

Most home and contents insurers allow you to "schedule" or "list" individual high-value items with a separate agreed value. This means naming specific items (e.g., "PSA 10 Charizard Base Set — $2,500") with their valuation, and paying a small additional premium for that specific item's coverage.

Typical cost: 1–3% of the item's insured value per year. On a $2,000 card, that's $20–$60/year for full replacement coverage. Contact your current insurer and ask specifically about "scheduling collectibles."

Limitation: You need to update schedules regularly as values change — a card that was $800 three years ago might now be $2,500, and an outdated schedule will underpay.

2. Collector-Specific Insurance (USA-Based, Some Serve AU)

Companies like Collectibles Insurance Services (CIS) and American Collectors Insurance primarily serve the US market but some policies extend to Australian collectors. These policies are designed specifically for collectibles with agreed value coverage, automatic acquisition coverage, and transit coverage. Research carefully whether an AU-based policy is available and legally compliant before purchasing international coverage.

3. Business Insurance (For Traders)

If you buy and sell cards commercially — at conventions, through eBay, or through a registered business — your home contents insurance almost certainly excludes your stock. You need commercial insurance: specifically a retail or general business policy with stock and public liability coverage. An insurance broker who specialises in small business can help structure this correctly.

Documenting Your Collection for Insurance

Regardless of which insurance route you take, documentation is essential for any claim to succeed. What you need:

  • Photographic record: Front and back photos of every card worth $100+. Store photos off-site (cloud storage).
  • Purchase receipts: eBay order history, shop receipts, convention purchase records
  • PSA/CGC grade certificates: Grading certs establish both identity and quality for high-value cards
  • Valuation records: Screenshot eBay sold listings for comparable cards at regular intervals (every 6–12 months)
  • Spreadsheet inventory: Card name, set, condition, estimated value, purchase price, purchase date

⚠️ Common mistake: Collectors wait until they have a claim to try to prove what they owned. By then, it's too late. Document your collection now, before anything happens.

Convention and Transit Coverage

Standard home contents insurance does not cover items while they're in transit or at a convention. If you take $5,000 worth of cards to Cardcon and they're stolen from your table or lost in a car break-in on the way home, your home policy almost certainly won't cover it.

Solutions:

  • Ask your insurer specifically about transit coverage for listed items
  • Consider adding a rider for "items away from home" if your collection goes to shows regularly
  • At conventions: use a quality lockable display case, never leave your table unattended, use satchels rather than standard bags for transport

Valuing Your Collection

Insurers require an agreed value for scheduled items. For Pokemon and One Piece cards, acceptable valuation evidence includes:

  • PSA Population Report combined with recent eBay AU sold listings for same grade
  • PriceCharting.com average sale price (recognised by some insurers)
  • Written appraisal from a reputable AU card dealer (strongest for high-value items)

Update valuations annually. Card values can move dramatically — Lugia EX went from $400 to $1,200+ in 18 months. An outdated agreed value means you're underinsured if values have risen.

The bottom line: if your collection is worth more than $3,000–$5,000, you are almost certainly underinsured under a standard policy. The fix isn't complicated or expensive — contact your insurer, ask about scheduling valuable items, document everything, and review annually.
