Your First Convention: Beginner's Guide to Buying & Selling Cards in Australia

J
John Ceron
February 14, 2026 · 14 min read

I remember my first card show. I walked in with $500 cash, no plan, and came home with a pile of cards I overpaid for and a vague sense that everyone else knew something I didn't. Four years and about 30 conventions later, I can tell you exactly what I wish someone had told me before that first show.

Whether you're going to buy, sell, or just browse, this guide covers everything you need to know about Australian card conventions.

The Australian Card Show Scene

Australia's card show scene has exploded since 2022. Here are the major events and series you should know about:

  • Card Fest — Australia's largest card convention series. Events in Melbourne, Sydney, Brisbane, and Perth. Multiple times per year. Usually at major exhibition centres. Entry is typically $10-15.
  • TCG World — Growing fast with events across the east coast. Good mix of big vendors and small collectors.
  • Collect-A-Con — Originally a US brand that's expanded to Australia. Premium feel with some big-name guests and grading company booths.
  • Local card shows — Smaller community-run events at RSL clubs, community halls, and school gymnasiums. Often free or gold coin donation. These are where the real deals happen because overhead is low and sellers are motivated.
  • Pop culture conventions — Supanova, Oz Comic-Con, PAX Aus — these always have card vendors but prices tend to be higher due to venue fees.

Follow @cardfestau, @tcgworld_au, and local Facebook groups for announcements. Most shows sell out their vendor tables weeks in advance, but general admission is rarely an issue.

What to Bring — The Essential Kit

Here's my convention go-bag, refined over dozens of shows:

For Buyers

  • Cash, cash, cash — Most vendors accept card (Square readers are everywhere now), but cash gets you 5-10% discounts. Hit the ATM before you arrive. I bring $300-500 minimum.
  • Phone with TCGPlayer/eBay — Your price-checking lifeline. Check recently sold listings, not active listings. There's a big difference between what people are asking and what's actually selling.
  • A small torch/flashlight — Essential for checking card surfaces under direct light. Overhead convention lighting is usually terrible for spotting scratches and print lines.
  • Jeweller's loupe (10x) — For checking texture on holos, spotting reprints, and verifying print quality. $15 on eBay and worth every cent.
  • Penny sleeves and toploaders — Vendors sometimes hand you cards loose or in flimsy sleeves. Protect your purchases immediately.
  • A backpack with zip closure — Not a tote bag. Not a messenger bag. A backpack that zips shut. Conventions are crowded and cards walk.
  • A want list — Written down or on your phone. It's easy to get overwhelmed by choice. Know what you're looking for before you walk in.
  • Water and snacks — Convention food is expensive and lines are long. Don't lose a deal because you left to grab a $7 sausage roll.

For Sellers

  • Everything above, plus:
  • Price labels or a price list — People won't ask if they have to ask. Make your prices visible.
  • A Square/bank card reader — You will lose sales without one. Set it up before the show.
  • Binder pages and a binder stand — For singles display. The easiest way to let people browse.
  • Tablecloth — Dark fabric works best. Makes your cards pop and looks professional.
  • Float (change) — At least $100 in $5s, $10s, and $20s. Someone will hand you a $50 for a $15 card in the first 10 minutes.
  • Business cards or social media QR code — People want to follow up after shows. Make it easy.

How to Negotiate Like a Local

Convention pricing is different from online pricing. Here are the unwritten rules:

Buying Tips

  1. Never offer on the first lap. Walk the entire floor first. See what's available and at what prices. You'll often find the same card at 3-4 different tables at different price points.
  2. Bundle for discounts. "I'll take these three for $X" works way better than haggling on individual cards. Vendors want volume — they paid for the table and want to move stock.
  3. Cash discount is real. "Would you do $X cash?" is the magic phrase. Vendors save 1.6-2% on payment processing plus get immediate liquidity. Most will drop 5-10% for cash.
  4. Last hour deals. Vendors don't want to pack up unsold stock. The final hour of a show is discount hour. Some sellers will do 20-30% off just to lighten the load.
  5. Don't lowball aggressively. The card community is small in Australia. Everyone knows everyone. If you offer $20 on a $100 card, you'll get remembered — and not in a good way.
  6. Reasonable offers are 10-20% below asking. That's the sweet spot. Some will say yes, some will counter, nobody gets offended.

Selling Tips

  1. Price at 5-10% below eBay sold prices. You're saving the buyer shipping and wait time. That's your value proposition. But don't give it away.
  2. Have a "firm" and "flexible" category. Some cards you know the price on. Others you'd love to move. Know which is which before someone asks.
  3. Be willing to trade. Many of the best deals at conventions aren't cash — they're trades. Know the value of what you have and what you want.
  4. Display matters. Cards in toploaders in a messy box get less attention than cards in binder pages with clear pricing. Presentation sells.

Spotting Fakes at Conventions

Fakes at Australian conventions are less common than online marketplaces, but they exist. Here's the quick check:

  • Texture test: Modern holos and full arts should have a textured surface you can feel with your fingernail. Fakes are almost always smooth. Run your finger across the card surface — if it's a holo/full art and perfectly smooth, be suspicious.
  • Light test: Hold the card up to your phone torch. Real Pokemon cards have a thin black layer in the middle of the cardstock that blocks most light. Fakes often let too much light through.
  • Colour saturation: Fakes tend to be either too saturated (colours are too vivid) or too washed out. Compare to a card you know is real.
  • The "feel": Sounds vague but after handling thousands of cards, you develop a sense for how a real card feels. The weight, the flex, the finish. If something feels "off," trust your gut and inspect further.

If you're ever unsure, just pass. There are plenty of legitimate cards at every show. No single deal is worth the risk of buying a fake.

Managing Cash and Safety

You're walking around with hundreds or thousands of dollars in cards and cash. Be smart:

  • Don't flash large amounts of cash. Use a money clip or wallet, not a wad of notes. Count money discretely, not on the table.
  • Keep expensive purchases in your bag, not in your hands. A toploader in your hand is easy to drop, bump into someone, or have snatched.
  • Go with a friend. Extra eyes, extra opinions on purchases, and someone to watch your stuff if you need to step away.
  • If selling, keep your cash box hidden. Under the table, in a locked box. Not sitting on top of your display.
  • Take photos of expensive cards before buying. Document the condition at the point of purchase. This protects both buyer and seller if there's a dispute later.

Setting Up as a Vendor

Thinking about getting a vendor table? Here's the reality check:

  • Table costs: $100-300 for most shows, $500+ for premium events like Card Fest or Collect-A-Con
  • Break-even: If your table costs $200, you need to sell $200+ just to cover that before you make a cent of profit
  • Bring more stock than you think. People browse. If your table looks thin, they'll skip you
  • Diversity sells. Don't just bring Pokemon. Have some One Piece, Dragon Ball, sports cards, graded cards. Different products attract different eyes
  • Arrive early. Setup often starts hours before doors open. Use that time to arrange your display, price everything, and network with neighbouring vendors

Convention Etiquette

A few things that'll make you a welcome regular:

  • Put cards back where you found them (or hand them back to the vendor)
  • Ask before touching graded slabs — the edges scratch
  • Don't hover at a table if you're not buying. Move along and come back later
  • If you pull out your phone to price check, that's fine — just be upfront about it. Most vendors respect informed buyers
  • Say thank you, even if you don't buy anything. The person behind that table is a collector just like you

First Timer Checklist

Here's your cheat sheet for your first show:

  1. ✅ Bring $300+ in cash
  2. ✅ Phone charged with price-check apps
  3. ✅ Want list written
  4. ✅ Backpack with penny sleeves and toploaders
  5. ✅ Small torch/loupe for authentication
  6. ✅ Water and snacks
  7. ✅ Walk the entire floor before buying anything
  8. ✅ Bundle cards for discounts
  9. ✅ Be polite, be prepared, have fun

Conventions are the best part of this hobby. There's nothing like the energy of a packed hall full of collectors, the thrill of finding that card you've been hunting, and the stories you'll hear from people who've been collecting since Base Set. Get out there and experience it. 🎴
