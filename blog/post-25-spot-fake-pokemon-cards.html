How to Spot Fake Pokemon Cards — 7 Easy Tests

By John C · Feb 15, 2026 · Card Collection Club

Fake Pokemon cards are everywhere — especially on marketplace apps and overseas sellers. Before you drop serious money on a card, here are 7 tests anyone can do to verify authenticity.

1. The Light Test

Hold the card up to a bright light (phone torch works). Real Pokemon cards have a dark layer in the middle — you should see a thin opaque line when light shines through. Fakes are often printed on single-layer cardstock and light passes straight through.

2. The Texture Test (Holo/Full Art Cards)

Modern Pokemon cards with holo or textured surfaces have a very specific feel. Run your finger across the card art. Real textured cards have a consistent, fine pattern you can feel. Fakes often feel smooth, plasticky, or have an uneven texture that doesn't match the art.

3. The Font Check

This catches most fakes immediately. Compare the text on the suspect card to a verified real card from the same set. Look for:

  • Font thickness — fakes often use slightly bolder or thinner text
  • Spacing between letters and lines
  • The energy symbols — fakes frequently get these wrong
  • The "©" and trademark text at the bottom

4. The Colour Saturation Test

Side by side with a known real card, fakes often have colours that are too saturated, too dark, or slightly off. The card back is the easiest check — the blue on the Pokemon card back is very specific. Fakes are usually a shade too dark or too bright.

5. The Rip Test (Last Resort)

Only do this with a card you're willing to destroy. Tear the corner — real Pokemon cards have a dark/black layer sandwiched between two white layers. Fakes are usually single-layer white cardstock throughout. Obviously, don't do this with anything valuable — save it for suspicious bulk cards.

6. The Bend Test

Real Pokemon cards have a specific flexibility. Gently bend the card — it should flex smoothly and spring back without creasing. Fakes tend to be either too rigid (thick cheap cardstock) or too flimsy (thin paper).

7. The Spelling & Set Symbol Check

Check for:

  • Misspelled words (common fake giveaway)
  • Wrong set symbol or missing set number
  • HP values that don't exist for that card
  • Attack damage that doesn't match the real card
  • "BASIC" vs stage info errors

Where Fakes Are Most Common

Be extra careful buying from:

  • Facebook Marketplace (no buyer protection)
  • Wish, Temu, AliExpress (most "Pokemon cards" are fake)
  • Random Instagram sellers without reviews
  • Markets and swap meets

Safe Buying Tips

  1. Buy from reputable local card shops — check our directory
  2. Use eBay with buyer protection (money back guarantee)
  3. Ask for close-up photos of the card front, back, and texture
  4. If the price seems too good to be true, it is
  5. Buy PSA/CGC graded cards for high-value purchases — they've already been authenticated

Stay safe out there. One fake Charizard can ruin your whole week. 🔍
