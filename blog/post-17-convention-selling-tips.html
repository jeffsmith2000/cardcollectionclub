Convention Selling Tips — Maximise Your Table Profit

By Ian N · February 18, 2026 · 9 min read

Selling cards at conventions and expos is one of the most rewarding — and exhausting — things you can do in this hobby. There's nothing quite like the buzz of a packed convention floor, the thrill of face-to-face haggling, and the satisfaction of clearing inventory while meeting fellow collectors. But it's also a logistical challenge, and getting it wrong can mean wasted time, money, and energy.

After years of selling at events across Australia — from Supanova and PAX Aus to local card shows and community markets — here are our battle-tested tips for maximising your table profit.

Before the Event

1. Know Your Event

Not all conventions are created equal. A gaming-focused event like PAX Aus will draw a different crowd than a pop-culture expo like Supanova or a dedicated card show. Research the event's demographics:

  • Gaming expos: Lean into competitive cards, meta decks, and sealed product for ripping.
  • Pop-culture cons: Focus on display pieces, graded cards, and nostalgia-driven product.
  • Dedicated card shows: Bring everything — these attendees are serious collectors and players.

2. Price Everything in Advance

This is non-negotiable. Every single, every sealed product, every graded slab should have a clearly visible price tag before you arrive. Attendees won't ask "how much?" — they'll just walk past. Use stickers, printed labels, or a clear price list displayed at eye level.

Pro Tip: Price your singles 5–10% below TCGplayer/eBay market value. Convention buyers expect a deal, and slightly below-market pricing drives volume. You save on shipping and fees, so the margin works out.

3. Prepare Your Float

Bring plenty of change. We recommend at least $200–$300 AUD in mixed notes and coins. Nothing kills a sale faster than "sorry, I can't break a fifty." Also set up Square, PayPal, or bank transfer as cashless payment options — many convention-goers prefer tap-and-go.

4. Pack Smart

  • Binder pages for singles (sleeved and sorted by set/price)
  • Display stands for graded cards and high-value items
  • Sealed product stacked neatly with clear pricing
  • Business cards or flyers with your online store details
  • Penny sleeves and toploaders for purchases
  • A tablecloth (black works best for cards — it's clean and professional)

At the Event

5. Your Display Is Everything

You have about three seconds to grab a passing attendee's attention. Your table needs to be visually striking:

  • Vertical displays: Use risers, acrylic stands, or even a small bookshelf to create height. Cards at eye level sell better than cards lying flat.
  • Hero products: Put your most impressive items front and centre. A PSA 10 Charizard or a sealed Evolving Skies box draws people in like a magnet.
  • Lighting: If the venue allows it, a small LED strip or battery-powered light can make your graded cards pop. Worth the effort.
  • Signage: A banner with your brand name, social handles, and website makes you look professional and memorable.

6. Engage Without Being Pushy

The best convention sellers are friendly, knowledgeable, and approachable — but not pushy. Greet people as they approach, let them browse, and be ready to answer questions. Share your knowledge genuinely: "That Umbreon VMAX is from Evolving Skies — one of the best modern sets they ever printed." Passion sells.

7. Bundle and Upsell

Bundling is your secret weapon. If someone's buying a $40 card, offer a related card at a discount: "I can do both for $65 — saves you ten bucks." People love feeling like they're getting a deal, and your average transaction value goes up.

Other bundling ideas:

  • "Pick any 3 cards from this binder for $20"
  • "Buy 2 booster boxes, get a free ETB"
  • "Spend over $100 and pick a free mystery pack"

8. Track Your Sales

Use a simple spreadsheet on your phone or a notepad to record every sale. This helps you understand what sells, at what price point, and gives you data for future events. Note which items drew the most attention, even if they didn't sell.

9. Handle Hagglers Gracefully

Convention culture involves haggling — it's expected and it's fine. Have a bottom line in mind for every item and be willing to negotiate within reason. A good framework:

  • Items under $20: firm pricing (the margin is too thin to negotiate)
  • Items $20–$100: 5–10% flexibility
  • Items over $100: up to 15% flexibility, especially for cash deals

10. End-of-Day Deals

In the last hour of the event, consider offering steeper discounts on remaining inventory. Anything you sell is product you don't have to pack up, ship home, or list online. Some sellers do a "last hour 20% off everything" — it creates urgency and clears stock.

After the Event

11. Follow Up

Anyone who took your business card or followed your socials is a warm lead. Post a thank-you on social media, tag the event, and remind people that unsold convention stock is available in your online store. The relationships you build at conventions often lead to repeat customers.

12. Crunch the Numbers

Calculate your true profit: total sales minus table cost, travel, accommodation, food, and the value of your time. This tells you whether the event was worthwhile and helps you decide which events to return to.

Quick Maths Example:
Table cost: $250 | Travel: $80 | Food: $40 | Total expenses: $370
Total sales: $1,800 | Cost of goods: $900 | Gross profit: $900
Net profit after expenses: $530
Not bad for a weekend!

Essential Gear Checklist

  • ✅ Priced inventory (sorted and sleeved)
  • ✅ Cash float ($200–$300)
  • ✅ Card reader (Square/PayPal)
  • ✅ Display stands and risers
  • ✅ Black tablecloth
  • ✅ Business cards/flyers
  • ✅ Penny sleeves and toploaders (for purchases)
  • ✅ Signage/banner
  • ✅ Phone charger/power bank
  • ✅ Water bottle and snacks (you won't want to leave your table)
  • ✅ Sales tracking sheet

Final Thoughts

Convention selling is part retail, part performance, and part community building. The money is great, but the connections you make — with fellow sellers, collectors, and fans — are what keep you coming back. Prepare thoroughly, present professionally, engage authentically, and you'll find that your table becomes a destination, not just a display.

See you on the convention floor, legends. 🎪
